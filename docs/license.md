## Why FSL and not MIT/Apache?

PocketDev is source-available today because of one specific risk: at launch, before a
community exists to defend the project, someone could fork the mobile app and publish it
to the App Store or Google Play under a different name. The FSL closes that gap with a
single, narrow restriction.

Everything else is open:
- Read, fork, and run the code freely
- Self-host the agent on your own servers
- Contribute code, open issues, build on top of it
- Use it for personal projects, internal tooling, research, or education

The one thing you cannot do: publish a derivative of the PocketDev mobile app to any
app store or distribution platform.

## The plan: transition to open source

FSL is not a permanent position. The goal is to relicense PocketDev to Apache 2.0 or MIT
once the project has a community large enough that the community itself is the best defense
against bad-faith forks. App store pirates thrive on obscurity — a well-known project with
an active community is naturally protected.

The FSL already has this built in: every version automatically converts to Apache 2.0 two
years after its first public release. But if the community grows faster than that, the
relicense can happen sooner.

## Contributing and the CLA

Contributors must agree to the [Contributor License Agreement](../CLA.md), which they do
automatically by submitting a pull request. The CLA is what makes the future Apache 2.0
transition possible — it ensures the Licensor can relicense without needing individual
sign-off from every contributor.
